The Business Model: Hybrid Closed-Loop Architecture

The evolution of American Express from a mid-19th-century express mail business into a global financial services and premium lifestyle powerhouse represents one of the most successful strategic pivots in corporate history. At the core of its contemporary operations is a unique "spend-centric" business model that distinguishes it from traditional banking institutions and open-loop payment networks like Visa and Mastercard.1

This integrated model, often referred to as a "closed-loop" system, allows the company to act simultaneously as the card issuer, the merchant acquirer, and the network processor.1 By maintaining a direct relationship with both the cardholder and the merchant, the organisation captures a larger share of the value chain and generates high-fidelity data that informs every aspect of its risk management, marketing, and product development.1

As of 2025, the company has successfully transitioned from a card-centric product provider to a membership-driven ecosystem.1 This strategic shift is anchored in the belief that financial services should be seamlessly integrated with lifestyle benefits, particularly in high-spend categories such as travel, dining, and entertainment.1

Closed-Loop Value Chain:
Cardholder ⟶ [AXP as Issuer] ⟶ Transaction ⟶ [AXP as Network] ⟶ [AXP as Acquirer] ⟶ Merchant

vs. Open-Loop (Visa/MC):
Cardholder ⟶ [Issuing Bank] ⟶ Transaction ⟶ [Network Switch] ⟶ [Acquiring Bank] ⟶ Merchant

In a traditional "four-party" or "open-loop" system, such as those operated by Visa and Mastercard, the network acts merely as a switch connecting the issuing bank, which manages the cardholder, and the acquiring bank, which manages the merchant.5 In contrast, the American Express "three-party" model internalises these roles, allowing the company to function as a self-contained ecosystem.3

By seeing both sides of every transaction, the company possesses superior visibility into consumer behaviour and merchant performance.1 This data advantage allows for more precise underwriting, as the organisation can correlate spending patterns at specific merchants with credit risk in real-time.1 Furthermore, because there are no intermediaries to share the transaction fees with, the company captures the full merchant discount rate, which typically ranges between 2.5 percent and 3.5 percent.3

The data generated from this network is the lifeblood of what management defines as the Membership Model. Instead of relying solely on traditional credit scores, the organisation uses its proprietary transaction data to build agentic AI models that provide hyper-personalised offers and concierge-style services.3

This infrastructure is currently undergoing a massive modernisation, with $5 billion in annual technology spending dedicated to migrating 100 percent of data and analytics processes to a third-generation cloud platform by 2027.10 For decades, the primary weakness of the American Express model was limited merchant acceptance compared to Visa and Mastercard, driven by the higher fees charged to merchants.1 However, as of 2025, the company has achieved 99 percent acceptance parity in the United States and has expanded its global network to over 170 million locations.1

The U.S. Consumer segment is the largest revenue generator.20 The Platinum Card refresh, which raised the annual fee while adding significant lifestyle and travel benefits, has proven that the customer base is relatively price-inelastic when the value proposition is sufficiently premiumised.3 Net card fees reached $10 billion in 2025, growing at double-digit rates for 30 consecutive quarters.4 This is no longer viewed as a cyclical trend but as a structural earnings driver.14

American Express is also a dominant force in the commercial payments space, with a presence three times larger than its nearest competitor in certain commercial categories.10 In 2025, the company was ranked number one in the J.D. The International segment is the fastest-growing area of the company, with spending up 12 percent on an FX-adjusted basis in the fourth quarter of 2025.10 The company has focused on premiumising its international portfolio, refreshing products in close to a dozen countries and expanding partnerships with global airlines like British Airways and Air France-KLM.10

Strategic partnerships are a cornerstone of the company's ability to drive volume and relevance. Delta Air Lines is the company's largest and most significant strategic partner. The Delta co-brand portfolio represented approximately 9 percent of worldwide network volumes as of late 2021 and accounted for 21 percent of worldwide Card Member loans.2 The current agreement runs through 2029.2 Beyond Delta, the company maintains critical relationships with Hilton and Marriott, with over $8 million in consumed room revenue to certain hotel groups in recent two-year periods, representing year-over-year growth of over 43 percent.26

The American Express Global Business Travel entity, which recently acquired CWT, reported revenue of $2.7 billion in 2025, a 12 percent increase over the previous year.28 The integration of the Center expense management solution and the launch of next-generation booking platforms powered by AI are aimed at revolutionising the agentic transformation of B2B travel.28

03

Customer Composition & Generational Shift

The most critical strategic development in the U.S. Consumer segment is the successful acquisition of younger, affluent customers. Millennials and Gen Z now constitute the largest share of U.S. consumer spending on American Express cards.18 The average age of a new U.S. Consumer Platinum cardholder is now 33, while the average for the Gold Card is 29.10

These HENRYs (High Earners, Not Rich Yet) are attracted by the integration of digital-first experiences and lifestyle rewards.3 For example, the integration with Resy has driven restaurant spending up by 20 percent among Platinum cardholders, as they use the platform to secure exclusive dining reservations.10

The company argues that while its merchant fees are higher, the average Amex cardholder spends significantly more than the average Visa or Mastercard user, making them a more valuable customer for the merchant to attract.2 The percentage of fee-paying products for U.S. consumer cardmembers is up 8 percentage points year over year as of 2025.10

The strategic decision to intentionally reallocate marketing dollars from lower-cost cash back products to premium offerings has fundamentally altered the company's margin profile.18 Platinum platform members spend approximately ten times more than entry-level cardholders, maximising the return on acquisition costs.19

Generational Flywheel

By targeting affluent Millennials and Gen Z early in their earning trajectory, American Express is building a long-duration revenue stream. These cardholders enter the ecosystem at the Gold Card level (avg. age 29), migrate upward to Platinum (avg. age 33), and are retained at a 95%+ rate1 — compounding both spend velocity and net card fee revenue across decades of customer lifetime value.

Revenue Streams & the Spend-Centric Pivot

The organisation's revenue mix is highly diversified, leaning heavily on non-interest income, which provides a degree of insulation from interest-rate cycles that traditionally affect retail banks.3 The revenue engine is powered by three primary cylinders: Discount Revenue, Net Card Fees, and Net Interest Income.

Revenue Formula (Simplified):

Discount Revenue = Billed Business Volume × Merchant Discount Rate (~2.5–3.5%)
Net Card Fees = Σ (Annual Fees per Card Tier × Active Fee-Paying Cards)
Net Interest Income = Interest on Revolving Balances − Cost of Funds

Discount revenue ≈ 55–60% of adjusted revenue9

Discount revenue remains the largest component, representing approximately 55 to 60 percent of adjusted revenue.9 In 2025, total billed business reached a record $1.67 trillion, an 8 percent increase over 2024.4 The growth in this segment is driven by the virtuous cycle of the spend-centric model: high-value rewards attract high-spending customers, whose volume justifies the higher fees merchants pay to access them.2

Net card fees reached $10 billion in 2025.4 Net interest income grew by 12 percent in the fourth quarter of 2025 alone.10 Crucially, the company's credit performance remains best-in-class, with a net write-off rate of 2.1 percent in late 2025, significantly lower than the industry average.1

Competitive Landscape & Industry Consolidation

The competitive landscape is shifting due to industry consolidation, most notably the 2025 merger between Capital One and Discover.8 This merger is seen as a proactive response to the Credit Card Competition Act, potentially allowing the combined entity to operate its own network and avoid the bill's mandates.8

As competitors follow the American Express playbook by focusing on premium customers and building proprietary networks, the pressure on Amex to innovate increases.10 The company aims to stay ahead through its massive tech budget and its unique lifestyle integrations that are difficult for traditional banks to replicate at scale.3

The competition for SME market share is also intensifying following acquisitions like Capital One's purchase of Brex.10 To stay ahead, American Express is integrating its expense management solutions directly into the platforms used by businesses, such as SAP Concur.28 By becoming an integral part of the business workflow, the company increases the switching costs for its commercial clients and secures a consistent stream of high-value transaction data.11

Economic Moat: Brand, Loyalty & Switching Costs

The durability of the American Express business model is protected by a wide economic moat composed of several reinforcing factors: network effects, high switching costs, and significant intangible assets.24

As the number of merchants accepting the card increases, the card becomes more valuable to consumers. Conversely, as the affluent cardholder base grows, more merchants are incentivised to join the network despite higher fees.24 The Membership Rewards program allows cardholders to accumulate points transferable to dozens of airline and hotel partners, creating a high degree of loyalty.3

Customers who have spent years accumulating points and attaining status through the Global Lounge Collection, which includes over 1,550 lounges, or concierge services are highly unlikely to switch to a competitor.24 This is reflected in the company's industry-leading 95 percent retention rate.1

The Blue Box brand is synonymous with trust, security, and service excellence.2 The strategic acquisition of membership assets like Resy and LoungeBuddy further deepens this moat, as these services create exclusive access points that cannot be matched by pure-play bank issuers or open-loop networks.1

Regulatory Environment & Industry Dynamics

Despite its strong market position, American Express faces a complex and evolving regulatory environment. The primary threats involve antitrust challenges to its business practices and legislative efforts to cap fees and interest rates.

The Credit Card Competition Act of 2026 is a major piece of legislation aimed at introducing mandatory routing competition into the credit card market to drive down swipe fees.8 Crucially, the current version of the bill includes an exemption for credit cards issued in a 3-party payment system model.8 Because American Express acts as its own network and issuer, it does not involve the same inter-institutional interchange structure as Visa and Mastercard.8

CCCA Asymmetric Advantage

If the bill passes, it could create a significant competitive advantage for Amex. While Visa and Mastercard issuers might be forced to reduce rewards to offset lower fee revenue, Amex could maintain its high-value rewards programme, potentially leading to a significant migration of premium customers to the Blue Box.8

In the landmark Ohio v. American Express case, the Supreme Court ruled in favour of the company, accepting the argument that the credit card market is a two-sided transaction platform where competition must be assessed by looking at both the merchant and cardholder sides simultaneously.13 However, in August 2025, a federal jury ordered the company to pay damages under state unfair acts laws, highlighting a growing risk from state-level competition laws.31

The proposed 10 percent credit card interest rate cap is viewed as a significant risk to the NII component of the business model. Management argues that such a cap could negatively impact small businesses and reduce the availability of credit lines, but the political climate remains a source of uncertainty.10

Risk Assessment

Concentration risk: The Delta Air Lines partnership accounted for approximately 9 percent of worldwide network volumes and 21 percent of worldwide Card Member loans as of late 2021.2 The agreement runs through 2029,2 and any deterioration in or loss of this relationship would have material financial consequences.

Regulatory risk: The proposed 10 percent interest rate cap would directly compress NII margins.3 State-level antitrust litigation is expanding — a federal jury in August 2025 ordered AXP to pay damages under state unfair acts laws.31

Competitive risk: The Capital One–Discover merger creates a potential new closed-loop competitor with a combined cardholder base capable of challenging AXP's premium positioning.8 Increased SME competition from Brex (via Capital One) threatens commercial share.10

Credit deterioration: While the 2.1% write-off rate remains below industry average,1 it has crept up from ~1.8% in FY 2023, warranting monitoring as the consumer credit cycle matures.

Fee elasticity limits: The Platinum Card refresh raised annual fees substantially. While take-up has been strong so far,10 there is a ceiling beyond which even affluent consumers will resist further price increases, potentially slowing net card fee momentum.

Macroeconomic exposure: Despite the premium customer mix providing counter-cyclical resilience, a significant economic downturn could slow billed business growth and new card acquisitions — metrics analysts are already monitoring.3

Cyclicality & Counter-Cyclical Resilience

The company's focus on the premium segment provides a counter-cyclical hedge. High-net-worth individuals and high-income earners tend to maintain their spending habits more consistently during economic downturns than the general population.1

Because the company's revenue is less dependent on interest income from carried balances, it is less exposed to the risk of "spread compression" in a declining interest rate environment.3 This mix of high-margin merchant discounts, rising net card fees, and ancillary travel services creates a resilience that is rare in the financial sector.9

The shift toward fee-based income is the structural key. Card fee revenue compounds independently of whether a customer carries a balance or pays in full.14 In a rising rate environment, NII expands. In a falling rate environment, the spend-centric model and card fee base cushion the decline — a structural asymmetry that distinguishes AXP from traditional bank issuers who are far more leveraged to the rate cycle.

Cyclical Exposure Profile:

Rate ↑ → NII expands, spend-centric revenue stable → Positive
Rate ↓ → NII compresses, card fees + MDR offset → Neutral-to-Mild Negative
Recession → Premium cohort more resilient, write-offs rise modestly → Mild Negative
Consumer boom → Billed business accelerates, all cylinders fire → Strong Positive

Investment Thesis

Core Thesis

American Express is a structurally advantaged compounder. The closed-loop architecture, spend-centric revenue model, and successful generational pivot create a business with high earnings visibility, durable pricing power, and a self-reinforcing flywheel that is difficult for competitors to replicate. The transition from a card company to a premium membership ecosystem has fundamentally re-rated the quality of its earnings stream.

The structural advantage of the closed-loop system, combined with a decade-long shift toward a membership-driven financial model, has created a business that is uniquely positioned to thrive in an era of digital, data-driven payments. By owning the customer relationship, the merchant network, and the processing infrastructure, American Express captures a "prestige premium" that its peers struggle to match.

As it moves into the latter half of the decade, the integration of agentic AI and cloud-native analytics will likely widen the gap between the Blue Box and its traditional banking competitors, ensuring that the organisation remains at the forefront of the global premium payments industry.
